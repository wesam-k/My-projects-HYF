Starting from the parking lot you will take the forest road up, direction Reschbergwisen. In the middle of the nice meadow follow the sign to Enningalm towards the right. Crank up on the wide road all the way to its highest point. After a short descent turn right on the road, direction Enningalm (by the big information table of Bavarian Saatsforsten). Shift up to your highest gear and work your way up on the steep platform. After that, just in a couple of meters, follow the hiking path to Enningalm.

For the good rides the fun begins now. From Enningalm thought the Hut area, ride in the direction Krottenköpfel (South). Turn right on the fork towards Linderhof. From there on you will find yourself on a funny, and easy to ride hiking path with many switchbacks turns, leading you to Rotmossalm. By the Rotmossalm stay left, and follow the path on the edge of the woods. On all the possible forks keep yourself right and after a steeper part, come a forest road turning to the left. After that, there is just a comfy path all the way to Garmisch where you can roll back to the parking lot in Burgrain.

Climbing

Zugspitze (2,962m): it's not just the highest point in Germany, but also a mystery that entices so many hikers, climbers and day-trippers. This is the Zugspitze. Remember though, to hike and climb mountains you must be very fit. But if you don't want to work that hard, you can combine climbing with a ride in the Zugspitzbahn cable car.

Germany's highest mountain is located southwest of Garmisch-Partenkirchen in the Wetterstein mountain range. The 2,962 meter (9,715 ft.) high summit forms the border between Germany and Austria. The first climb took place in 1820 and was commissioned by the "Royal Bavarian Topographic Bureau".
What you need to bring to the hike to Zugspitze:

  1. Water (2 – 3 litres) for the first day
  2. Snacks and optional lunch for both days
  3. Blister plaster and emergency blanket and first aid kit
  4. Ear plugs and a sleeping mask for a good sleep
  5. thin sleeping bag
  6. Warm clothes in layers because it is cold on the peak
  7. scarf & hat for the peak
  8. Changing clothes for the next day and for the summit

Routes to Zugspitze What you lack on the summit you may enjoy on your way up. There are two normal climbing routes, the south route from Reintal and the north-east route from Höllental (this latter means Hell valley).
in summer time it requires only a basic walking gear. There are two huts on that route, Reintalangerhutte (1.366 m), and Knorrhütte (2052 m).

Far more attractive appears to be the Höllental route. You will start from Hammersbach, a village at around 750 meters above the sea. This route includes all you may wish to experience, walk through man-made tunnels, a long glacier walk, several lengthy ferratas, rock scramble, there may be icy areas along ferrata sections.
There is also a mountain hut Höllentalangerhutte (1379 m) on the way up, and most of the climbers spend a night there. You will have to pay an entrance fee (3 Euro in 2010) at the gate in Hoellentalklamm which is at around one hour walk from Hammersbach.

Sking

Bavaria boasts more than 100 ski regions, world cup locations and spectacular pistes – from the Allgäu to the Berchtesgaden Region.
However, family skiing is a particular favourite in Bavaria, such as in Mitterfirmansreut, an idyllic village in the Bayerischer Wald (Bavarian Forest).

40 kilometres of pistes, 4 valley descents and rustic ski huts guarantee lots of snowy fun for the whole family in Garmisch-Partenkirchen. Three ski mountains unite to form the Garmisch-Classic ski area: Hausberg, Kreuzeck and Alpspitze offer winter sports enthusiasts an extensive range of pistes – newcomers and professionals, skiers and snowboarders, youngsters and veterans – everyone finds their perfect descent here. From gentle to a 92% gradient, Garmisch-Partenkirchen has everything. 40 piste kilometres of all levels of difficulty make this a family-friendly ski area. And those looking for a serious challenge between the wide blue and red pistes can find what they want on the Kreuzeck. Free fall on the Kandahar

The local mountain with its range of gentle to moderate pistes, on the other hand, is ideal for children and inexperienced skiers and snowboarders, while the pistes below the Alpspitze vary in terms of difficulty, but are hugely impressive with their magnificent views and wonderful mountain panorama. As the Alpspitze lies above the tree line, there are uninterrupted views of the charming Alpine scenery.

It counts as one of the most legendary descents in the Alps: the Kandahar descent. With one section at a gradient of 92% it is one of the steepest pistes in the world. The top skiers come down it as part of the Ski World Cup. Relaxed ski fun – for beginners too

Tobogganing

Tobogganing is a popular sport in Bavaria. Kids hurtle down the village slopes.
Grown men vie for glory on giant horn sleds.

Long tobogganing runs offer several minutes of downhill fun,Get those runners hot as you speed from the mountain station down into the valley. In Upper Bavaria, not only can you slide down the Wallberg on one of Germany’s longest toboggan runs, but you can also enjoy some floodlit tobogganing at night on the Blomberg.

In Upper Bavaria, not only can you slide down the Wallberg on one of Germany’s longest toboggan runs, but you can also enjoy some floodlit tobogganing at night on the Blomberg.

Clouds of smoke rise up out of a chimney on the edge of Leubach in the Rhön. They come from a cast-iron stove which heats a workshop that at first glance doesn’t look like anything special. After their day’s work is done, Andreas Weber and Alexander Hergenhan ply an unusual craft here: They build traditional sledges known as Davos sledges. “These are sledges with crescent-shaped runners, which have slats attached to their top ends”, explains Alexander Hergenhan. The 31-year-old is a master joiner and the great-grandson of the founder of “Weber-Schlitten“. He has been involved in the manufacture since 2013. Together with his uncle, Andreas Weber, who has been building Davos sledges as a sideline since 1980, in recent years he has breathed fresh life into the workshop, set up in 1924.

They used to make the sledges out of beech but nowadays we use native ash”, says master joiner Weber. Why? Ash has longer fibres and therefore lasts longer than beech. Each sledge represents three hours’ work, but it takes over three months from start to finish. “We start by boiling up the wood for the runners in a large cauldron to make it pliable”, says Andreas Weber, explaining a key stage in the process. We then use a bending machine to get it into the correct shape. After two or three months of drying time, both men mount the stands on the runners then add the seating slats.
The Davos sledge is a genuine classic on Bavaria’s toboggan runs, such as the one from Wallberg down to the town of Tegernsee. It is one of the most beautiful runs in the country. You slide through a snow-covered winter landscape for half an hour, breathe in the fresh air and enjoy the speed of the runners. Winter can be so beautiful.

Walking

Just walking: That’s all some outdoor sportspeople want to do, even in winter.
In Bavaria, the Bayerischer Wald (Bavarian Forest) is the ideal destination. Numerous trails are cleared and rolled and there are specially marked winter walking trails. There’s even the allure of a mountain peak …

Who wants to sledge in Upper Bavaria, which is on the Tegernsee gold right! Because here there is a whole row of toboggan runs, especially one of the longest natural toboggan runs in Germany on the Wallberg near Rottach-Egern. For a total of 6.5 kilometers - that's about half an hour - tobogganing fans can plunge downhill to enjoy themselves. So that everything goes right and no one ends up in the lurch, the train, which can be conquered by pedestrian or Wallberg train, is prepared and checked daily. If you do not have your own sledge, you can rent one at the valley station of the Wallbergbahn. Since the Wallberg route is not completely safe, wearing a helmet is also recommended for experienced drivers.
